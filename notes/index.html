1. What the Data Are

The input is a 2D matrix of rapid-scan EPR absorption spectra with dimensions $N_\text{field} \times N_\text{time}$. Each column is a complete absorption-mode EPR sweep taken at one time point; each row traces the amplitude at a single field value over time. The experiment is a photo-triggered kinetic EPR measurement: a laser pulse perturbs a spin-labeled protein, and the EPR spectrum evolves as labeled residues transition between conformational states with different inter-spin distances.

Three kinetically distinct time phases appear in the data:

  1. Pre-laser baseline ($t < t_\text{on}$) — static, no perturbation; pure intrinsic lineshape.
  2. Rising phase ($t_\text{on} \le t \le t_\text{off}$) — fraction in perturbed state grows with time constant $\tau_1$.
  3. Decaying phase ($t > t_\text{off}$) — fraction relaxes back toward baseline with time constant $\tau_2$.

$t_\text{on}$ and $t_\text{off}$ are fixed by experimental design and are not fitted parameters.

2. The Forward Model

The simulation is implemented across three chained functions: alpha_heaviside_tau → double_gaussian → simulate_matrix.

2.1 Kinetic mixing function

At each time point $t_i$, alpha_heaviside_tau computes the instantaneous fraction of spin pairs in the perturbed conformation:

$$ \alpha(t) = \beta + \alpha_0 \left[ \Theta(t - t_\text{on})\,\Theta(t_\text{off} - t)\, \bigl(1 - e^{-(t - t_\text{on})/\tau_1}\bigr) + \bigl(1 - e^{-(t_\text{off} - t_\text{on})/\tau_1}\bigr)\, \Theta(t - t_\text{off})\, e^{-(t - t_\text{off})/\tau_2} \right] $$

where $\Theta$ is the Heaviside step. The decay amplitude starts exactly where the rise ended — the recovery does not assume the rise saturated to its maximum.

$\alpha_0 = n_\text{resp}/(n_\text{resp}+1) \cdot (1 - \beta)$ is the maximum responding fraction. The internal optimization parameter is $n_\text{resp}$ rather than $\alpha_0$ directly, because the natural constraint $\alpha_0 \in [0, 1)$ maps to $n_\text{resp} \in [0, \infty)$ — an open interior problem that the optimizer handles without running into a hard wall at 1. $\beta$ is the pre-existing perturbed fraction before the laser fires (a DC offset on $\alpha(t)$).

2.2 Bimodal distance distribution

double_gaussian evaluates the spin-pair distance distribution $P(r, t)$ over a discrete $r$-grid at each time point:

$$ P(r, t) = A \left[(1 - \alpha(t))\,\mathcal{N}(r;\, r_0, w_0) \;+\; \alpha(t)\,\mathcal{N}(r;\, r_1, w_1)\right] $$

where $\mathcal{N}(r; \mu, \sigma) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}\,e^{-(r-\mu)^2/(2\sigma^2)}$. When $\alpha(t) \to 0$ (pre-laser or long after recovery), spins reside near $r_0$ (one conformational state); when $\alpha(t) \to 1$ (at peak perturbation), spins reside near $r_1 = r_0 + \delta$ (the other state). $P(r, t)$ has shape $(N_r, N_t)$.

2.3 Pake-weighted spectral profile

The pre-computed lookup table pake_data has shape $(N_\text{field}, N_r)$: each column $K(B, r)$ is the Pake dipolar broadening function for inter-spin distance $r$. Marginalizing over the distance distribution gives the dipolar contribution to the spectrum:

$$ S_\text{pake}(B, t) = \int P(r, t)\, K(B, r)\, dr \;\approx\; \sum_j P(r_j, t)\, K(B, r_j)\,\Delta r $$

In code: pake_profiles = (pake_data @ P_r_t) * dr, shape $(N_\text{field}, N_t)$. Multiplying by $\Delta r$ makes the discrete sum a proper quadrature approximation, so the amplitude $A$ is independent of the number of $r$-grid points used.

2.4 Convolution with intrinsic lineshape and field shift

The measured EPR absorption is the convolution of the dipolar broadening pattern with the single-label (intrinsic) lineshape — a standard result for a powder-averaged ensemble:

$$ \text{model}(B, t) = S_\text{pake}(\cdot,\, t) \;*_B\; L_\text{intrinsic}(B) $$

This is implemented via FFT along the field axis for all time points simultaneously (rfft → multiply → irfft). A rigid field shift shift $= n$ is applied as a linear phase ramp in the frequency domain before the inverse FFT, which is differentiable and avoids interpolation artifacts:

$$ \tilde{S}(k) \;\to\; \tilde{S}(k)\,e^{\,2\pi i k \cdot (n \cdot N_\text{field}/2)} $$

The result is sliced from the center of the full linear convolution output to extract the $N_\text{field}$-point window that aligns with the measured field axis.

3. The Chi-Square Objective

The residual at each $(B_i, t_j)$ point is windowed, noise-normalized, and flattened before lmfit forms the scalar $\chi^2 = \|\mathbf{r}\|^2$:

$$ r_{ij} = \frac{[\,d(B_i, t_j) - m(B_i, t_j)\,]\cdot w^\text{field}(B_i)\cdot w^\text{time}(t_j)} {\sigma_\text{noise}\,\sqrt{N_\text{total}}} $$

where $N_\text{total} = N_\text{field} \times N_\text{time}$ and $\chi^2 = \sum_{ij} r_{ij}^2$.

3.1 Field window $w^\text{field}$

A super-Gaussian (order 4) centered on the spectrum, tapering smoothly to zero in the wings where signal is negligible:

$$ w^\text{field}(B) = \exp\!\left[-\tfrac{1}{2}\left(\frac{B}{\sigma_\text{field}}\right)^4\right] \qquad (\sigma_\text{field} = 15\,\text{G}) $$

The high order (4 vs. 2 for a standard Gaussian) gives a flat top over the spectral region and sharper roll-off, reducing contribution from noise-only wings without a hard cutoff.

3.2 Time window $w^\text{time}$

A double-sided exponential peaking at $t_\text{off}$ (the moment of maximum kinetic displacement):

$$ w^\text{time}(t) = \exp\!\left(-\frac{|t - t_\text{off}|}{w}\right) $$

The width $w$ is chosen so the weight falls to 10% at $t = t_\text{max}$:

$$ w = \frac{t_\text{max} - t_\text{off}}{-\ln(0.10)} \approx \frac{t_\text{max} - t_\text{off}}{2.303} $$

3.3 Noise estimate $\sigma_\text{noise}$

$\sigma_\text{noise}$ is estimated from the quiet time regions only — before the laser fires ($t < t_\text{on}$) and well after signal has decayed ($t > t_\text{off} + 5\,\text{s}$) — to avoid contaminating the estimate with rapidly-changing signal near $t_\text{on}$. Within those columns, Gaussian-smoothing along the field axis removes the (spectrally smooth) EPR signal and leaves only noise:

$$ \epsilon(B) = d(B, t_\text{quiet}) - \left[\text{Gauss}_{\sigma^*} \;*_B\; d(\cdot, t_\text{quiet})\right](B) $$ $$ \sigma_\text{noise} = \text{std}[\epsilon] $$

The smoothing width $\sigma^*$ is selected automatically via an L-curve. As $\sigma$ increases, $\text{std}(d - \text{Gauss}_\sigma(d))$ rises from near zero (smooth tracks the noise) through a knee where the EPR signal is absorbed but noise is not, then rises again once $\sigma$ exceeds the EPR linewidth and signal structure leaks into the residual. The Menger curvature of the $(\log\sigma,\,\text{std})$ curve identifies this knee:

$$ \sigma^* = \operatorname*{arg\,max}_\sigma \kappa(\log\sigma,\,\text{std}) $$

The smoothing width $\sigma^*$ is selected automatically each run and the diagnostic plot is saved as noise_smooth_sigma_lcurve.png in the fit output folder. The left panel shows std(residual) vs. $\sigma$ and the right panel shows the Menger curvature; the vertical red line marks $\sigma^*$. A well-chosen $\sigma^*$ sits clearly at the knee before the curve flattens.

3.4 Why $\chi^2_\text{min} \approx 0.09$ and not 1

The $1/\sqrt{N_\text{total}}$ factor makes $\chi^2$ a mean of squared normalized residuals rather than a sum. For independent, unit-variance data the expected value at the optimum would be 1. In practice:

$$ \chi^2 = \frac{\sum_{ij}(w_{ij}\,\epsilon_{ij})^2}{\sigma_\text{noise}^2\,N_\text{total}} \approx \frac{\sigma_\text{actual}^2}{\sigma_\text{noise}^2} \cdot \overline{w^2} $$

where $w_{ij} = w^\text{field}(B_i)\cdot w^\text{time}(t_j)$ and $\overline{w^2} = N_\text{total}^{-1}\sum_{ij}w_{ij}^2$. Both windows taper substantially below 1 over most of the data (the time window reaches 10% at the extremes; the field window falls to zero in the wings), so $\overline{w^2} \ll 1$. With $\overline{w^2} \approx 0.09$, the minimum chi-square reflects the window geometry, not a miscalibrated noise estimate. The confidence interval threshold in Section 4.3 is derived to be invariant to this rescaling.

3.5 Log-normal soft priors and the L-curve for $\tau_\text{prior}$

Four parameters have log-normal priors appended as additional residual components:

$$ \mathbf{r}_\text{prior} = \frac{1}{\tau_\text{prior}}\begin{pmatrix} \ln(w_0/w_0^\text{prior}) \\ \ln(w_1/w_1^\text{prior}) \\ \ln(\tau_2/\tau_2^\text{prior}) \\ \ln(\beta/\beta^\text{prior}) \end{pmatrix} $$

These are concatenated onto the data residual vector. In the residual vector each prior element is $O(1/\tau_\text{prior})$ when a parameter deviates by one e-fold — much larger than any individual data residual element, which is $O(1/\sqrt{N_\text{total}})$. However, $\chi^2$ sums all squared elements, and there are only 4 prior terms versus $N_\text{total} \sim 10^4$ data terms. So in $\chi^2$ terms:

$$ \chi^2_\text{data} = \sum_{ij} r_{ij}^2 \sim O(0.09) \qquad \text{(at optimum)} $$ $$ \chi^2_\text{prior} = \sum_{l=1}^{4} r_{\text{prior},l}^2 \sim O\!\left(\frac{4}{\tau_\text{prior}^2}\right) \approx 0.28 \quad \text{when each param is 1 e-fold from its prior} $$

The prior contribution is therefore comparable in scale to the data chi-square — intentionally so. It is strong enough to prevent degenerate solutions for parameters the data is insensitive to (especially $w_0$, $w_1$ at large $r$), but it does not overwhelm the data: at the optimum the parameters sit near their prior centers and the prior contribution is near zero. The prior only exerts meaningful force when a parameter drifts significantly from a physically reasonable value.

Choosing $\tau_\text{prior}$ — the value 3.8 was selected using an L-curve analysis: the optimizer scanned $\tau_\text{prior}$ over a grid and recorded both the data chi-square and the summed prior deviation at each value. Plotting prior deviation vs. data chi-square traces an L-shaped curve; the corner of the L is the point of maximum curvature, representing the best balance between data fidelity and prior constraint.

4. Profile Likelihood Matrix

The profile likelihood matrix is a corner plot showing (a) how $\chi^2$ changes as each free parameter is forced away from its optimum (diagonal panels), and (b) how all other parameters co-vary in response (off-diagonal panels). It is the primary tool for assessing parameter identifiability and inter-parameter correlations.

4.1 Scan procedure

  1. Start from the LSQ best-fit parameter vector $\hat{\theta}$.
  2. For each free parameter $\theta_k$, construct a scan grid over its allowed bounds — log-spaced if $\theta_k^\text{max}/\theta_k^\text{min} > 20$ with $\theta_k^\text{min} > 0$ (applies to $n_\text{resp}$, $\tau_1$, $\tau_2$, linewidths), linear otherwise. 15–20 points per parameter.
  3. Bi-directional scan: scan outward from $\hat{\theta}_k$ in both directions separately. The previous step's fitted parameters seed the next step's optimizer, so each solve starts only one small move from a good solution and avoids being trapped after crossing a high-$\chi^2$ barrier.
  4. At each scan point: fix $\theta_k$, free all other parameters, run lmfit.minimize (Trust Region Reflective via SciPy least_squares, handles bounds natively). Record $\chi^2$ and all co-parameter values.

$r_1 = r_0 + \delta$ is not scanned independently — its profile is read from the $\delta$ scan by recording the implied $r_1$ at each step after $r_0$ has co-varied freely. $n_\text{resp}$ is scanned internally but all plots display $\alpha_0 = n_\text{resp}/(n_\text{resp}+1)\cdot(1-\beta)$.

4.2 Effective sample size $N_\text{eff}$

Adjacent field points are correlated because the EPR lineshape is broad relative to the field step size. We estimate $N_\text{eff,field}$ from the autocorrelation function (ACF) of the smooth-subtracted noise along the field axis. For each time column, compute $\epsilon_j(B) = d(B,t_j) - \text{Gauss}[d(\cdot,t_j)](B)$ and its normalized ACF $\bar{\rho}(\ell)$ averaged over all time columns. Truncate at the first zero crossing to avoid negative-lobe bias:

$$ N_\text{eff,field} = \frac{N_\text{field}}{1 + 2\displaystyle\sum_{\ell=1}^{\ell^*} \bar{\rho}(\ell)} $$

We use smooth-subtracted noise (not model residuals) because model residuals contain systematic mismatch structure that shortens the apparent ACF and would underestimate $N_\text{eff}$.

On the time axis, only three kinetically independent observations exist: pre-laser baseline, rising phase ($\tau_1$), and decaying phase ($\tau_2$). $t_\text{on}$ and $t_\text{off}$ are fixed, not estimated from the data. Hence:

$$ N_\text{eff} = N_\text{eff,field} \times 3 \;\approx\; 52 $$

4.3 Confidence interval threshold

Wilks' theorem: for a sum chi-square with independent unit-variance data, the 95% CI boundary satisfies $\Delta\chi^2_\text{sum} = 3.84$. Our chi-square is a mean, $\chi^2 = \chi^2_\text{sum}/N_\text{total}$, and the data are correlated with $N_\text{eff}$ effective independent observations. Translating Wilks to our units:

$$ \Delta\chi^2 = \frac{3.84}{N_\text{eff}} $$

But as shown in Section 3.4, $\chi^2_\text{min} \approx \overline{w^2} \ne 1$ due to window weighting. Both $\chi^2_\text{min}$ and any profile variation $\Delta\chi^2$ scale by the same factor $\overline{w^2}$, so the threshold must be rescaled by the same amount to live in the same amplitude as the profile. Since $\chi^2_\text{min}$ directly measures $\overline{w^2}$, the correct threshold is:

$$ \boxed{\Delta\chi^2_\text{threshold} = 3.84 \times \frac{\chi^2_\text{min}}{N_\text{eff}}} $$

Equivalently: the invariant statement is the fractional rise in chi-square, $\Delta\chi^2 / \chi^2_\text{min} = 3.84 / N_\text{eff}$. This form is also correct when $\sigma_\text{noise}$ is mis-estimated by a constant factor, because such an error scales both $\chi^2_\text{min}$ and $\Delta\chi^2_\text{profile}$ identically, leaving their ratio unchanged.

4.4 CI bound estimation

For each diagonal profile, CI bounds are found in two stages:

  1. Direct interpolation: if two adjacent scan points straddle the threshold $\chi^2_\text{min} + \Delta\chi^2_\text{threshold}$, linearly interpolate the exact crossing. Most reliable when the threshold is crossed within the scan range.
  2. Parabola extrapolation: if the threshold is not crossed within the scan range, fit a parabola $\chi^2(\theta) = a(\theta - \theta^*)^2 + \chi^2_\text{min}$ to the 12 lowest-$\chi^2$ scan points and solve analytically: $\theta^* \pm \sqrt{\Delta\chi^2_\text{threshold}/a}$. Valid when the profile is approximately quadratic near the minimum.

Fitting is done in $\log_{10}(\theta)$ space for log-spaced parameters to symmetrize the parabola. Extrapolated bounds are clipped if they lie more than 1000× outside the scan range, and noted as "off-plot" if they fall outside the plotted x-range.

4.5 The $\pm 1\sigma$ curvature band

The parabola fit also gives the local curvature $a$. Using the same relative Wilks scaling as the 95% CI, the $\pm1\sigma$ threshold is $$\Delta\chi^2_{1\sigma} = \frac{\chi^2_\text{min}}{N_\text{eff}}$$ (the 68.3% quantile of $\chi^2(1)$ is 1, so this is exactly the 95% threshold divided by 3.84). The band is $\theta^* \pm \sqrt{\Delta\chi^2_{1\sigma}/a}$ and is shown as blue shading on diagonal panels. Using the raw $\Delta\chi^2 = 1$ threshold (the textbook Wilks value for a properly normalised $\chi^2$) would be incorrect here because $\chi^2_\text{min}/N_\text{eff} \ll 1$ due to the field and time window weighting — it would produce a band far wider than the 95% CI. The rescaled threshold keeps the $\pm1\sigma$ band consistently narrower than the 95% CI. If the two are comparable in width the profile is nearly parabolic and the Wilks approximation is reliable; if the profile is very flat both will be wide.

5. Limitations

$N_\text{eff}$ uncertainty: The effective sample size is estimated, not exact. Sources of error include:

  • Smooth width choice (1.5 G) for noise extraction — factor-of-2 change alters the ACF shape.
  • ACF truncation at first zero crossing is a heuristic; slow oscillations can cause premature truncation.
  • $N_\text{eff,time} = 3$ is a physical argument, not measured.

Rough uncertainty in $N_\text{eff}$: a factor of 2–3×, propagating directly to CI width. The $\pm1\sigma$ curvature band (Section 4.5) uses the same relative scaling as the 95% CI and so carries the same $N_\text{eff}$ uncertainty, but its width relative to the CI is a useful check on profile parabolicity.

Model mismatch: The fit residuals contain both random noise and systematic model-mismatch structure (visible as faint spectral features in the residuals that co-vary with the signal). Chi-square at the optimum therefore has two contributions: $\chi^2_\text{min} = \chi^2_\text{noise} + \chi^2_\text{mismatch}$. The profile likelihood CI inherits both — it describes the set of parameter values consistent with the data given both the noise level and the model's inability to perfectly describe the lineshape. This makes the CIs conservatively wide, which is the honest interpretation when the model is not perfect.

7. Standard Analysis Workflow

A step-by-step guide for fitting a new dataset from scratch. Every step is controlled by flags at the top of pakeGlobalFit_v3.py.

Noise pipeline (runs at the start of every fit and every profile scan)

Two quantities are estimated before any fitting: the noise amplitude sigma_noise and the effective sample size N_eff. Both share the same auto-selected field-smoothing width.

1. Auto-select smoothing width via L-curve — _smooth_sigma_lcurve(data_quiet, field) scans field_smooth_sigma over a log-spaced range (~0.1–5 G). For each candidate value it computes std(data - gaussian_filter1d(data, sigma_pts, axis=0)) on the quiet-region data (see below), builds the curve (log field_smooth_sigma, std), and finds the knee via Menger curvature. The knee value is stored as _smooth_sigma (typically ~0.7 G). A two-panel diagnostic is saved to fits/noise_smooth_sigma_lcurve.png.

2. Noise amplitude — estimate_sigma_noise(data, field, t=t_full, t_on=t_on, t_off=t_off) first masks the time axis to quiet regions: t < t_on OR t > t_off + 5 s, giving data_quiet (shape: n_field × n_quiet_times). It then applies the auto-selected _smooth_sigma and returns:

  • sigma_noise = std(data_quiet − gaussian_filter1d(data_quiet, sigma_pts, axis=0))
  • noise_2d — the 2-D noise array (field × quiet-times) used for diagnostics
  • _smooth_sigma — the L-curve-selected smoothing width in Gauss
sigma_noise is passed to fit_function() and divides all data residuals so that $\chi^2 \approx 1$ for a well-calibrated fit. It has no effect on CI bounds (cancels in the relative Wilks ratio) but sets the scale of tau_prior regularisation.

3. Effective sample size — estimate_n_eff(data_full[:, ::skip_times], field=field_interp, field_smooth_sigma=_smooth_sigma) uses the full dataset (all time points, subsampled by skip_times) because field-axis correlation is a spectrometer property independent of signal content. Steps:

  1. Subtract gaussian_filter1d(data, sigma_pts, axis=0) using the same _smooth_sigma to get the noise array.
  2. For each time column $j$: compute the normalised field-axis ACF $\hat{\rho}_k^{(j)} = \tfrac{\sum_i x_i x_{i+k}}{\sum_i x_i^2}$ via np.correlate(col, col, mode="full"), averaged over all valid columns.
  3. Sum to the first zero-crossing lag $k_0$: $N_\text{eff,field} = N_\text{field} / (1 + 2\sum_{k=1}^{k_0}\hat\rho_k)$.
  4. Multiply by n_time_independent = 3 (pre-laser baseline, rising phase $\tau_1$, decaying phase $\tau_2$): $N_\text{eff} = N_\text{eff,field} \times 3$.
Printed as: N_eff = XX.X (N_eff_field=YY.Y × N_eff_time=3 fixed)

4. Wilks threshold — $\Delta\chi^2_\text{95\%} = 3.84 \times \chi^2_\text{min} / N_\text{eff}$, where $\chi^2_\text{min}$ is re-evaluated at the stored LSQ parameters. Printed as: chi2_min(LSQ) = X.XXX Δchi2_threshold(95%) = X.XXX.

Step 1 — Fast exploratory fit

Set flags at the top of the script:

SKIP_TO_EMCEE       = False
REDUCE_COMPUTATION_SPEEDUP = True   # coarse r-grid, fewer field/time points
RUN_PROFILE_MATRIX  = False
PROFILE_TAU  = False
REFINE_FROM_SEED    = False

Run the script. The pipeline is:

  1. Noise estimation (see box above) — prints Auto-selected field_smooth_sigma = X.XX G (L-curve knee) and Estimated σ_noise = X.XXXXX. Saves fits/noise_smooth_sigma_lcurve.png. Verify the knee is at a plausible value (~0.5–2 G); if the curve has no clear knee, the quiet-region mask may be too short.
  2. Basinhopping — global stochastic search; saves intermediate best parameters to fits/.fit_checkpoint.repr after each improving step.
  3. LSQ polish — Trust Region Reflective local minimisation from the basinhopping result. Saves final parameters to fits/.fit_params_lsq.repr.
  4. Plots — raw_imshow.png, fit_imshow.png, residue.png, slice.png, slice_windowed.png (shows SL, DL, DL×W(B), and W(B)). Check that the fit residuals are small and structureless.

The goal here is a plausible starting parameter set, not a publication fit. The coarse grid makes this run fast.

Step 2 — Calibrate $\tau_\text{prior}$ via L-curve

Set flags:

SKIP_TO_EMCEE      = False
REDUCE_COMPUTATION_SPEEDUP = True   # fast is fine for this scan
PROFILE_TAU = True           # runs the tau_prior L-curve scan

The script loads the LSQ result from Step 1, then scans tau_prior over a log-spaced range (typically $10^{-1}$ to $10^2$). At each value it re-runs the LSQ and records:

  • $\chi^2_\text{data}$ — the data-only residual (without prior penalty)
  • $\chi^2_\text{prior}$ — the prior deviation term $\sum_p (\ln\theta_p - \ln\theta_p^0)^2 / \tau_\text{prior}^2$
These form the L-curve. Menger curvature is computed at each interior point and the maximum-curvature corner is the optimal $\tau_\text{prior}$: strong enough to prevent degeneracy, weak enough not to dominate the data. Read the printed value and hardcode:

TAU_PRIOR = 3.8   # (or whatever the L-curve corner gives)

Re-derive this for each new sample if SNR or linewidths differ significantly. A gap between the all-priors run and a w0/w1-only run indicates one prior conflicts with the data — investigate before proceeding.

Step 3 — Production fit at full resolution

Set flags:

SKIP_TO_EMCEE       = False
REDUCE_COMPUTATION_SPEEDUP = False  # full r-grid, full field/time resolution
PROFILE_TAU  = False
RUN_PROFILE_MATRIX  = False
REFINE_FROM_SEED    = False

Run the script. Basinhopping + LSQ at full resolution. Output goes to fits/. Key things to check:

  • noise_smooth_sigma_lcurve.png — confirm _smooth_sigma is consistent with the fast run. A large change (e.g. 0.7 G → 2 G) suggests the noise or signal structure changed between resolutions.
  • sigma_noise printed value — should be stable to ~10% between runs at different resolutions.
  • Residual plots — structureless residuals confirm the model is adequate.
  • fits/.fit_params_lsq.repr — saved parameter dict used by all subsequent steps. Do not delete unless starting over.
If the profile matrix (Step 4) later reports a lower $\chi^2$ than the stored LSQ, the code prints a !!! warning, updates chi2_min_lsq, and saves an improved seed. Set REFINE_FROM_SEED = True and re-run once to polish from that seed without basinhopping. Reset to False afterwards.

Step 4 — Profile likelihood matrix for confidence intervals

Set flags:

SKIP_TO_EMCEE      = True    # skip basinhopping; load saved LSQ params
RUN_PROFILE_MATRIX = True    # run the full corner-plot scan
REDUCE_COMPUTATION_SPEEDUP = False

The script:

  1. Loads fits/.fit_params_lsq.repr.
  2. Runs the noise pipeline (see box above) — prints _smooth_sigma, sigma_noise, N_eff_field, N_eff_total, chi2_min_lsq, and delta_chi2_threshold. Verify these are consistent with Step 3.
  3. For each free parameter: scans over a grid of ~20 values spanning the parameter bounds (log-spaced if the bound ratio exceeds 20×, linear otherwise). At each grid point the parameter is fixed and all others are re-optimised with TRF. The scan runs bi-directionally (outward from the LSQ value, warm-starting each step from the previous result) to avoid basin-hopping artefacts. The LSQ point itself is injected into every scan's DataFrame so the profile minimum is correctly anchored.
  4. After all scans, checks whether any scan found a lower $\chi^2$ than chi2_min_lsq. If so, prints !!! and saves an improved seed (see Step 3 warning).
  5. Plots profile_likelihood_matrix.png (corner plot). Each diagonal panel shows: raw $\chi^2$ profile (black), 95% CI threshold (red dashed, $\Delta\chi^2 = 3.84\,\chi^2_\text{min}/N_\text{eff}$), $\pm1\sigma$ band (blue, $\Delta\chi^2 = \chi^2_\text{min}/N_\text{eff}$, rescaled Wilks), and the LSQ value (red dot). Off-diagonal panels show parameter co-variation traces.
  6. Prints the CI table: Parameter | LSQ | CI_low | CI_high (95%, N_eff=XX).

What to check:

  • Each diagonal minimum should sit at or very near the red LSQ dot. A shifted minimum means the LSQ is in a local basin — use the !!! seed and REFINE_FROM_SEED.
  • Open bounds (<scan_min / >scan_max) are physically meaningful: e.g. $\tau_1 \to 0$ (faster than time resolution) or $n_\text{resp} \to$ bound (model insensitive at saturation).
  • The $\pm1\sigma$ blue band should be narrower than the 95% CI red lines. Large separation indicates an asymmetric or non-parabolic profile — the Gaussian approximation would be misleading for that parameter; use the CI bounds.
  • For derived quantity $r_1 = r_0 + \delta$, the CI is computed from the joint profile over the scan of $\delta$ (with $r_0$ free to co-vary), so it is correctly narrower than naive propagation of the individual CIs.
